Background & Context

The dataset used in the FiveThirtyEight story "Why We're Sharing 3 Million Russian Troll Tweets" comprised a total of 2,973,371 tweets from Twitter handles linked to the Russian "troll factory" called the Internet Research Agency. The original data set spanned November 2017 to June 2018, and was gathered by Clemson University researchers using custom searches on Social Studio, a firm owned by Salesforce.

A selection of nearly 400,000 tweets were gathered from this original data set, and uploaded to Kaggle. The dataset includes active links, with caution advised. These selections were analyzed on multiple dimensions: Account holder language, account holder region, most common hashtags, and most common string terms. In the following pages, these dimensions are evaluated for meaning.

Data Cleaning

Cleaning and formatting the data is a step necessary to produce accurate and meaningful visualizations. For this step, the timestamps were formatted to a readable data object. Some data was cleaned from the original Kaggle set, including 'publish_date' column, which has very little data overall, and any row where 'region' or 'language' are not defined. To enhance the readability of the charts, some changes were made: If the account had than 10,000 followers, they have been grouped into a language category "other". This enhances the readability of the chart. Likewise, if the account had less than 2000 followers, the region is grouped as "other". Once cleaned, the observations are reduced from nearly 400,000 rows to 112427 rows.

Highcharter Visualizations

Table 1: This highcharter graph shows daily follower counts based on account language or region. Bots may avoid languages with limited readership to maximize impact and avoid bot detection. Western European languages dominate these results, aligning with the organization's goal of disrupting societies outside Russia and Asia.

Table 2: This highcharter table shows the languages of accounts with > 2000 followers. "Other" includes: Bengali, Bulgarian, Catalan, Czech, Estonian, German, Greek, Hebrew, Hindi, Hungarian, Indonesian, Italian, Japanese, Korean, Kurdish, Latvian, Macedonian, Malay, Pushto, Russian, Serbian, Simplified Chinese, Slovak, Slovenian, Somali, Swedish, Tagalog (Filipino), Thai, Traditional Chinese, Ukrainian. This also reflects that, despite being attributed to Russian bots, most account holders are in the United States.

Word Cloud & Hashtag Visualizations

Table 3: This wordcloud (limit 80 terms) displays most frequent strings, with stopwords set to English. Some single-character Cyrillic terms were unavoidable. These terms, recurring 100 times or more, emphasizes political jargon like trump, president, state, politics, and obama. Contrasting concepts (man/woman, life/killed, black/white, stop/go, never/now, weight/fitness), as well as direct commands (take, see, watch, think, vote, look, know) reveal the operation's objective: influencing readers through polarizing concepts and encouraging direct action.

Table 4: The hashtag network (limit 75 terms) reveals term frequency, with a min_freq set of 0.4 to exclude infrequently-used hashtags. Again, this highlights politically-polarizing terms, ie maga, trumptrain, blm, fergusonremembers. Bots integrate hashtags like soundcloud, hiphop, and meme trends to appear as genuine human users. They also employ 'rage bait' content on topics like the Second Amendment ('2a') and All Cops Are Bastards ('acab') to manipulate followers. Numerous hashtags with unclear significance, like 'arre -- ara', 'alis -- lis', 'amelin -- ameliss', and 'ade -- aded', may result from lost context or gibberish generated by bots.

Reflection

The selection of this dataset was motivated by my recollection of the unfolding circumstances surrounding Russian bots on social media, a topic I avidly followed during that period. My curiosity was piqued by the prospect of delving into the data to discern the insights it might reveal.

Of particular note was the prevalence of missing elements within the dataset, prompting the exclusion of over 200,000 rows due to the absence of language or region information. This raises questions, as it appears counterintuitive that accounts would lack such fundamental attributes. The exploration of these omissions warrants further inquiry to unveil the underlying reasons for this anomaly.

The use of flexdashboard posed significant challenges, particularly surrounding the successful rendering of the file. I ran into the problem of graphs being compressed to a single row of pixels, or appearing as code instead of illustrative figures. Mitigating this issue necessitated extensive rearrangement and independent research. Ultimately, my decision was to compartmentalize sections into their own pages, a strategy that proved effective in resolving the rendering challenges. I look forward to delving deeper into flexdashboard in future projects.
