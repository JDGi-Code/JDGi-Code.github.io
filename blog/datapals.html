Elevating Performance Through Sleep

Exploring the Impact of Consistent Routines on Well-being and Functionality.
A data-centric project aiming to investigate the relationship between consistent sleep routines and its positive effects on daily performance.

May 2024

Authors: Rauzat S., Tanvi K., Kate S., J.D. Gi

Medium Article

Problem Statement

This research seeks to explore the effect of sleep on four chosen performance areas: productivity, stress resilience, alertness, and overall energy. As college students who are academically-focused, we were interested in this health data to gain insight into how sleep can be leveraged to help us meet our academic goals. Individually, we have different sleep habits and needs, which allows us to explore this health data through different lenses.

Our Research Questions

  1. How does the overall sleep data correlate with these four performance areas?
  2. How does sleep schedule consistency impact these four performance areas?
  3. How does sleep duration affect the performance outcomes differently?
  4. Do external factors (exams, stress at home, health issues) impact our performance or sleep?

These research questions have evolved for specificity and focus. We define "consistent" as maintaining a similar sleep duration across at least three consecutive days. Therefore, inconsistent sleep schedules must be the opposite - random durations, and without consistency. By examining the differences in outcomes between these two types of sleep schedules, we hope to gain understanding of the performance benefit of consistent sleep.

Data Sources

Two data streams were collected: Automatic sleep data tracked by our PI devices nightly, and manually recorded performance metrics. Each of the following were tracked daily:

  • Productivity: A personal feeling of having completed obligations, tasks, or achieving goals, measured by output (completed tasks within a given timeframe) or outcome (evaluates the quality of the output).
  • Stress resilience: Ability to cope with/bounce back from stressful situations, measured by assessing emotional mood (ie confidence, optimism, etc).
  • Alertness: Capacity to concentrate on routine tasks and remain attentive to responsibilities (remembering appointments, being alert throughout the day, etc). It is acknowledged that natural attention span rarely exceeds 10 minutes and "zoning out" to a certain degree is a normal part of daily life.
  • Overall Energy: General level of vitality and physical/mental stamina for a given day (willingness to take on unplanned tasks, ease of moving from one task to another, general sense of fatigue).

Scale

These areas were measured using a scale from 1 to 5, reflecting personal feelings about each of these areas according to the individual researcher.

Productivity:

  • 1 Very low - Unable to accomplish any tasks.
  • 2 Low - Accomplished some tasks slowly and with effort.
  • 3 Moderate - Accomplished baseline tasks required for the day
  • 4 High - Met most obligations with a feeling of motivation
  • 5 Peak - Every goal for the day was eagerly met and completed.

Stress Resilience:

  • 1 Very low - Feeling overwhelmed by stress highly impacting mood.
  • 2 Low - Easily overwhelmed by minor challenges
  • 3 Moderate - Mood remains fairly stable for the majority of the day
  • 4 High - Able to calmly address challenging stressful situations.
  • 5 Peak - Positive mood when confronted with daily stressors.

Alertness:

  • 1 Very low - Easily distracted, struggling to remain engaged.
  • 2 Low - Somewhat easily distracted, difficult to maintain focus.
  • 3 Moderate - Can concentrate on tasks with occasional attention lapse.
  • 4 High - Feeling attentive and focused with minimal effort.
  • 5 Peak - Super sharp and focused, quick & efficient mental processing.

Overall Energy:

  • 1 Very low - Difficult to concentrate or engage; lethargic.
  • 2 Low - Tired, low enthusiasm or motivation, but performing basic tasks.
  • 3 Moderate - Completing daily tasks but with fatigue or and needing breaks.
  • 4 High - Motivated and able to complete tasks with focus.
  • 5 Peak - Super enthusiastic to tackle all the tasks for the day.

Our group chose to record performance metrics in two ways: Use of manual tracking in a weekly digital document, and optionally via a creative bullet journal (bujo) page. Those who chose the bujo method had the opportunity to personally engage with the data, and will further reflect on how this format enhanced their research experience.

Solution

Data Collection Process & Comprehensive Analysis

Gathering data allows us to explore the link between sleep and performance variables. By visually analyzing this data, we aim to uncover correlations. Examining the impact of sleep consistency on performance helps us understand its effects. Comparing our findings determines if consistent sleep truly enhances specific performance areas. Studying the relationship between sleep duration and wellbeing can reveal optimal sleep patterns for performance maximization. Recognizing confounding variables like exams, stress, and health issues is key to understanding how our lifestyles influence our performance. Join us as we delve into our research to discover the benefits of structured sleep schedules. Take a moment to pause and reflect alongside us on our outcomes, and consider what you yourself might do to take action to improve your sleep for optimizing your daily performance.

Limitations of Data Collection Approach for Accurate Answers

Environmental Factors: Although stimuli like noise and living space differences can affect sleep quality and/or performance outcomes, our data does not account for these environmental influences, and that may introduce bias into our results.
Manual Data Entry: Manually-recorded performance data introduces a risk of human error, though we endeavored to be accurate as possible in this regard.
Subjectivity of Data: Due to the personal nature of our own performance assessments, quantification of performance data will vary from person to person.
Device Reliability: Battery failure and data synchronization problems may lead to missing or incomplete data, possibly affecting the accuracy of the analysis.
Data Limitations: Our study includes a small group of college students, which restricts the generalizability of the findings. Our results may not apply to a broader audience of researchers doing similar studies, however the personal nature of our findings provides some latitude in this regard.
Brief Duration: The data collection period of 6 weeks may be insufficient to draw definitive conclusions. A longer study may provide for a more robust analysis. With this in mind, we regard our research as an exploratory undertaking, rather than being solely outcomes-focused.

Data Pipeline

Collection, Extraction, and Analysis Procedure

Performance data, collected manually using a common template, were ranked on a scale of 1 to 5. We included any comments related to our personal circumstances affecting our performance as we saw fit. Automated sleep data, collected with our PI tracking devices, was exported to CSV files at the end of the collection period. Using Python Pandas, the data was combined and cleaned to prepare for analysis.

Summary of Data Cleaning Process

Because our team used both AppleWatches and Pixel devices, the sleep tracking information formats were not consistent. AppleWatch's XML format and sleep duration in hours and minutes conflicted with Pixel's CSV format and duration (formatted as a two-digit 'sleep score'). Consequently, we needed a solution to standardize the formats before analysis. We changed the scope of Research Question 2 to focus on sleep duration in minutes, supported by both device types, and arranged our data as CSVs with a common structure with six columns: Observation date, four performance areas (productivity, stress resilience, alertness and overall energy) and sleep duration in total minutes. This allowed for consistency and eased the process of preparing the data for analysis.

Evolution of our Research Endeavor

As none of us had ever tracked sleep in this way before this endeavor, there were things learned through trial and error and supported successful data collection. First, we didn't realize we needed to set alarms for AppleWatch to track sleep data, and therefore set alarms each day, including weekends, to ensure data consistency. It also became apparent that it was important to set a consistent daily time to track performance. At first, we recorded performance metrics at random times of day, but after discussion considered this may bias our results. Therefore, we also set device reminders to ensure manual data collection consistency as well.

Q1: Analysis of Overall Data

These plots provide a big picture look at the data we have collected over the past 6 weeks, broken down by metric tracked with separate colors for each researcher. The y-axis for each graph ranges from 0–600 minutes of sleep and the x-axis ranges from 1–5 for each metric except for alertness, which ranges from 2–5 because no group members reported a 1 in that category. Each point represents the rating of a specific metric after how many minutes of sleep the group member got the previous night. The data is clearly varied for each group member and between group members. For example, it seems as though JG and Tanvi got less sleep (in minutes), on average, than Kate and Rauzat. Overall, each metric did not yield a highly significant trend for every group member, as we may have expected. Judging from this data, it is clear that a longer study is needed to determine any significant correlations between sleep and our chosen metrics.
While we see evidence of sleep habits having an individualized impact, it also reflects the subjective nature of our performance scores. JG did not record anything below a 2 for the duration of the research period. This could skew results slightly, and further research may benefit from less subjective data collection. For instance, instead of each individual recording their personal perspectives, an interviewer could ask about performance metrics and probe for more details before recording data on the 1–5 scale.
With regard to data combining challenges, due to the fact that the data frames were equal in number of rows and columns, and each frames' columns maintained a consistent naming convention, the data combined seamlessly.

Q2: Evidence of Benefits of Consistent Sleep

Based on analysis of each researcher's sleep and performance data, the benefits of consistent sleep vary depending on the person. In JG's analysis, it is clear that sleep consistency is absent, so has no effect on her performance. On the other hand, Rauzat's performance data is relatively consistent with her sleep duration, particularly in the second half of the data collection period, so there is a correlation between sleep consistency and performance, particularly productivity.
For both Tanvi and Kate, there were some metrics which seemed to be contingent on sleep duration and others which weren't, but no evidence of consistency benefits. In general, each of these statements would be made more conclusive with a larger data collection period. However, these conclusions remain valid given the data that has been collected. Despite these variations in certain performance metrics between group members, stress resilience levels were very inconsistent for all four group members when compared to sleep duration. This is likely due to the number of outside factors which are involved in determining one's stress resilience levels. Overall, it is interesting to note how sleep duration is very individualized in how it benefits each member of our group.

Challenges in Achieving Consistent Sleep for 3+ Days in a Row

There were many unforeseen challenges with getting blocks of consistent sleep for at least three days in a row. A common issue for everyone in our group was the fact that Spring Break occurred early on in the data collection process. As such, all of our schedules were no longer consistent and a few of us went on vacation so flight times impacted our sleep consistency. Additionally, mental illness created challenges in maintaining a consistent sleep schedule. Depending on the severity of depression and anxiety symptoms, it was difficult for some of us to control when we went to bed and when we woke up on certain days. Also, one of our group members suffers from insomnia which also increases the difficulty of maintaining a consistent sleep duration. Beyond our personal circumstances, a varying workload makes it difficult to get consistent durations, as some days require more time dedicated to work and therefore being awake more time than others.

Q3: How Sleep Duration Impacts Specific Performance Areas

Each researcher's sleep duration reflects different lifestyle habits and these scatter plots indicate, ceteris paribus, sleep duration may impact performance in an individualist sense. Duration seems to have the most impact on three of the four performance areas (alertness, stress resilience, and energy). The plots for duration and each performance area show that three of four researchers have a similar trend line for alertness, stress resilience, and energy. Using the following scatterplot grid, with each column representing the full data set of a single researcher, we can get a closer look at how sleep duration impacts each performance area.
The visualizations above show the relationship between sleep duration and performance data. The Pearson coefficient, used to estimate the linear relationship between two quantitative measures, has been provided. This is more informative for our purposes than the t-test, which may be more appropriate for discovering which of the four variables is most significantly influenced by sleep. In this case, given the small data set and our purposes, Pearson is likely more useful. 

  • Kate: Productivity is slightly negatively correlated to sleep duration (-0.183). For this researcher, it may be that a sense productivity is linked to something other than being 'well rested.' Alertness (0.113), however, had a slightly positive correlation, indicating more sleep helps them to achieve a higher level of focus. On the other hand, sleep didn't have any significant impact on their stress resilience (-0.014) and energy levels (-0.027)
  • JG: Productivity (-0.062) is only slightly negatively correlated, and alertness (0.053) not significantly correlated, which indicates these variables are not meaningfully influenced by sleep duration. Sleep duration is slightly negatively correlated with stress resilience (-0.184), however more data would be needed to gain any meaningful insight. Overall energy (0.09) is slightly positively correlated, but not significantly. As overall sleep duration does not fluctuate significantly throughout the experiment, and the data is limited, it would be difficult to draw conclusions as to what this means for this individual, however stress resilience decreasing with more sleep may be influenced by external factors.
  • Rauzat: Productivity (0.176) and energy ( 0.112) show a slight positive correlation with the sleep duration which indicates that a bit more sleep could slightly improve these areas. Stress resilience (-0.063) and alertness (0.037) do not show a significant correlation with sleep, suggesting that sleep duration didn't have any significant impact on the stress resilience and alertness. As there is only a slight correlation between sleep and all variables, more data would benefit the research to determine how sleep duration impacts performance for Rauzat.
  • Tanvi: There's a slight negative correlation for productivity (-0.114) and alertness (-0.096), meaning more sleep might make this individual feel sluggish. Again, the data set is small so this is a broad statement. Stress resilience (-0.473) shows a stronger negative correlation with sleep duration. This could mean that the individual sought more sleep as a result of stress, like an illness or other outside factor. Their energy (0.332) shows a stronger positive correlation with sleep, suggesting that more sleep leads to higher energy levels, which contradicts the results for stress resilience somewhat.

As most researchers' sleep duration trends differ overall by no more than 30 mins, more data is needed before any conclusions can be made. As these are trend analyses, the date labels associated with documented life events are not included. What we can see, however, is it's not as simple as 'more sleep equals better everything.' There is high contrast and individualism which imply that external factors impact the dependent variables and sleep duration alone might not statistically determine outcomes.

Q4: Sentiment Analysis on Reflection Notes

Sentiment analysis was performed on our reflection notes using the R package wordcloud2. First a word cloud was created with a frequency of 80 to reflect the most common words used in our reflections. Although some data scientists argue that word clouds are not the best tools for analysis, the image provides a helpful starting point to get an overview of our feelings during the research.
Given the brevity of our study, the quantity of textual notes was limited. To supplement our data set, reflections from our previous milestone were included to increase the wordcount to just under 1600 words. This low quantity made analysis somewhat difficult, as the notes also lacked depth of descriptive words and in some cases. Additionally, the English stopwords dictionary was supplemented with additional words to improve the wordcloud sample: "week", "sleep", "slept", "like", "bit", "day". A deeper look at our researcher notes can shine some light on where the most commonly-appearing words come from:

  • Mood: "I had a lot of wins this week that helped me keep my mood high… it seems this is determined more by encounters rather than amount of sleep"
  • Stress: "I feel like there's never really a way to feel completely 'safe' from academic stress; as much as you try to control stress, you can never be sure what is going to happen."
  • Consistent: "Maintaining a consistent sleep schedule has helped me to plan my day as I know the amount of time I need to be awake."
  • Flight: "Had to wake up at 1:45 am to get on a flight … slept on a plane and then bed when I got there … adjusting to time change"
  • Performance: "This week I felt for the first time the incredible realization that soon my time at UMass will be over. I believe this affected my performance areas overall this week in a highly positive way."
  • Feel: "If anything, I feel as though my overall energy scores are most indicative of my sleep duration/quality as it has a direct effect on how I feel. As students we are required to meet deadlines whether we feel productive and energized or not, so my circumstances seem to affect my tracked metrics just as much as my sleep does."
  • Energy: "Toward the end of the week, a sudden academic responsibility forced me to expend the last shred of energy."

These personal insights provide context for our considerations that occurred throughout the study about external stimuli including travel, positive encounters, classroom obligations, optimism, and time management. The insight we gain from this is that while sleep is a passive activity, there are many factors that contribute to quality other than that time the person physically enters and exits their state of sleeping. Due to this reality, it may be impossible to meaningfully curate the "optimum sleep schedule." Nevertheless, exploration of our recorded thoughts and feelings helped us to meaningfully consider how outside factors play a significant role in our performance from day to day, which may inform our behavior modifications, going forward.
